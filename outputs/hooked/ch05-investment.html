Chapter 5: Investment

Practical ⏱ ~9 min read

The Big Idea

The final phase of the Hook isn't about getting a reward — it's about doing a small bit of work after the reward. When users invest effort, they store value inside the product: content they've created, data the algorithm has learned about them, followers they've built up, reputation they've earned. This stored value makes the product better on every future visit. And crucially, it can't be taken to a competitor. The investment is what turns a one-time user into a long-term habit.
🔍 How the Author Got There
❌
Common assumption: Products retain users by making it hard to leave. Why it fails: That's hostility, not habit. Users resent feeling trapped and leave the moment a better product appears.
❌
Next guess: Just make the reward so good they keep coming back for it. Why it fails: A competitor with a better reward wins every time. Pure reward is a race to the bottom.
❌
Third guess: Ask users to invest upfront — more skin in the game. Why it fails: Effort before reward feels like work, not value. Users abandon the product before the habit forms.
✅
The real insight: Ask for small investment AFTER the reward, and make sure that investment improves their next experience. Users invest willingly because they get something back — a product that knows them better, a social graph that belongs to them, a reputation they've earned. The moat grows from the inside out.

Key Concepts

Investment Is Not About Now — It's About Next Time

Investment is effort the user puts in after receiving a reward. It doesn't pay off immediately. It loads two things for the future: stored value (the product improves) and the next trigger (a reason to return). This is what separates the Investment phase from just "more friction."

The timing is critical. The ask must come right after a reward — users are in a primed state. Ask too early (before the reward), and it feels like work. Ask too late, and the moment has passed.

Think of it like: leaving a better bookmark. You don't get rewarded for putting the bookmark in, but next time you open the book you start exactly where you need to be — and the reading is better for it.
In practice: Twitter asks new users to follow 5 accounts before leaving. The user gets no immediate reward for following. But their next visit has a personalised feed — the investment just paid off.
💡 Investment is planting seeds, not harvesting them. You never water seeds and expect fruit the same day.
The IKEA Effect — Effort Creates Attachment

Most people assume we value things because they're high quality. But research shows we value things because we helped create them — regardless of quality. This is the IKEA Effect: furniture you assembled yourself feels more valuable than identical pre-assembled furniture, even when it looks exactly the same.

The implication for product design is powerful. When users build something inside your product — a playlist, a profile, a portfolio — they've transferred emotional ownership to it. Leaving means abandoning something they made.

Think of it like: a sandcastle you built vs. one someone else built. You'll check yours more, protect it more, and feel worse when the tide takes it.
In practice: Fantasy sports leagues. Users spend hours drafting teams, making waiver moves, managing lineups — for a team that doesn't physically exist. The effort makes it feel real. Deeply real.
💡 Labor = Love. The more someone does, the more they value what they made. Effort is not a barrier — it's a bonding agent.
The Five Forms of Stored Value

Not all investment is equal. Eyal identifies five types of stored value that products can accumulate. The more personal and non-transferable the value, the stronger the habit moat.

ContentPins, notes, posts, photos you've created. Your Evernote notebooks, your Pinterest boards. Pure you.
DataPreferences, listening history, tagged emails. Spotify's Discover Weekly gets better because it's been watching you for years.
FollowersA social graph you built one connection at a time. Moving to a new platform means starting from zero.
ReputationeBay seller rating, Stack Overflow score, Airbnb host reviews. Years of behaviour compressed into a number — and it lives here, not there.
SkillProficiency that only works in this product. A Photoshop expert doesn't want to relearn everything in a new tool. A veteran gamer doesn't want to restart their character.
💡 CDFRS — Content, Data, Followers, Reputation, Skill. Any product that builds even one of these is building a moat that grows with every use.
Loading the Next Trigger

Every investment action should do double duty: store value AND plant the seed of the next hook cycle. If the investment doesn't bring the user back, it's just friction with extra steps.

Think of it like: sending a letter. Writing the letter is effort (investment). But sending it creates the expectation of a reply — which is the trigger that brings you back to the mailbox.
In practice: Sending an email is an investment. The moment you hit send, you've loaded a trigger — you'll check back to see if they replied. The act of investing created the next reason to return.
In practice: Following someone on Twitter is investment. Their next tweet is a trigger that lands directly in your feed. You created the trigger yourself.
💡 Investment is the last step that sets up the first step of the next cycle. If your investment doesn't point back to a trigger, redesign it.
Stored Value vs. Switching Costs — A Critical Distinction

Most people assume stored value and switching costs are the same thing — both keep users from leaving. But they're completely different in how users feel about them.

Switching costs are punishments. The product makes leaving painful — your data is locked, your files can't be exported, the process is deliberately hard. Users resent this and leave as soon as a strong-enough alternative appears.

Stored value is a reward. The product gets genuinely better as you use it. Your Spotify Discover Weekly is better than anyone else's because it's uniquely yours. Users don't want to leave because their next experience here is better than their first experience anywhere else.

💡 Switching costs create resentful prisoners. Stored value creates loyal residents. One is a cage, the other is a garden. Design the garden.

Stories & Case Studies

Snapchat Streaks: The Simplest Investment Imaginable

A Snapchat streak is just a number — a count of consecutive days two people have exchanged snaps. There is no inherent value in the number. But Snapchat users describe losing a 200-day streak as genuinely upsetting, sometimes heartbreaking. Snap even introduced "Streak Freeze" — a paid feature that lets you protect your streak if you miss a day — because the anxiety of losing streaks was so intense it was driving churn. The streak is stored value in its purest form: personalised, visible, impossible to transfer, and emotionally loaded far beyond its functional worth. Snap didn't engineer loyalty — they engineered loss aversion. The investment is microscopic (one snap per day), but the accumulated value feels enormous.

LinkedIn's Profile Completion Bar: Investment Dressed as Progress

LinkedIn shows every user a "Profile Strength" meter — Beginner, Intermediate, Expert, All-Star. It's almost always incomplete. Each gap (no headline, no summary, missing skills) is a small investment invitation. The genius is that every piece of information users add — their work history, their recommendations, their skills endorsements — is both stored value (their professional identity, years in the making) and a trigger loader (recruiter views, connection suggestions, "People Also Viewed" traffic). A complete LinkedIn profile cannot be recreated in an afternoon elsewhere. The investment is deep, personal, and cumulative. No rival professional network has ever successfully lured users away en masse, not because LinkedIn's features are superior, but because what users have built there belongs to them in a way nothing can replicate.

Chapter Takeaways

  • Place your investment ask immediately after the reward moment — that's when users are most willing to put in effort.
  • Keep the ask tiny. A big investment before the habit is formed feels like work. One follow, one setting, one short action.
  • Map which of the five stored value types (CDFRS) your product builds. If the answer is none, design for at least one this sprint.
  • Every investment action should load a trigger. If users invest but have no clear reason to return, the loop is broken.
  • Stop designing switching costs. Design stored value instead — users who stay because leaving feels wasteful will leave. Users who stay because their experience is uniquely theirs will stay forever.

Apply This Week

▶ Action 1: Walk your product's core flow. Find the moment the user receives their main reward. Now look at what happens in the 30 seconds after. Is there an investment ask? If not, add one — a follow, a save, a preference, a piece of content. It must be frictionless and it must make their next visit better.

▶ Action 2: List every piece of stored value your product currently holds for a power user. Then ask: is any of it transferable to a competitor in under 5 minutes? If yes, that's not real stored value — it's just data. Find the one thing only your product accumulates and double down on it.
