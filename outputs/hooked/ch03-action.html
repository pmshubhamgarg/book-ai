Chapter 3: Action

Practical ⏱ ~9 min read

The Big Idea

A trigger is useless if the user doesn't act on it. The Action phase is about making the desired behavior as effortless as possible. Dr. BJ Fogg's formula — B = MAT (Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Trigger) — tells us that all three must be present simultaneously. But the biggest lever for most products isn't motivation; it's ability. Removing friction is almost always more effective than increasing desire.

Key Concepts

The Fogg Behavior Model: B = MAT

Any behavior requires three things firing at the same moment: a Trigger (covered in Ch. 2), sufficient Motivation, and sufficient Ability. If any one is missing, the behavior won't occur — no matter how strong the other two are.

Think of it like: a car engine. You need fuel (motivation), a working engine (ability), and a key turn (trigger). A full tank means nothing if the engine is seized.
In practice: Your phone rings. You don't answer because (a) it's buried in your bag — low ability, or (b) you think it's spam — low motivation, or (c) it's on silent — no trigger. Same outcome, three different root causes.
💡 When users don't act, diagnose which leg of MAT is broken before trying to fix anything.
The 3 Core Motivators

Fogg identifies three fundamental levers of human motivation, each with a positive and negative side:

  • Seek pleasure / avoid pain
  • Seek hope / avoid fear
  • Seek social acceptance / avoid rejection
In practice: Obama's 2008 "Hope" poster activated the hope motivator. Motorcycle helmet ads showing head injuries activate fear. Budweiser ads showing friends together activate social acceptance.
💡 Motivation is expensive to change. Ability is cheap to improve. Always start with ability.
The 6 Elements of Simplicity (Ability)

Fogg's six factors that make an action easy or hard. For any given user at any given moment, one of these is the bottleneck. Find it and remove it.

TimeHow long the action takes
MoneyFinancial cost to the user
Physical EffortBody labor required
Brain CyclesMental effort and focus needed
Social DevianceHow normal the behavior feels
Non-RoutineHow much it disrupts existing habits
Think of it like: a garden hose with a kink. The water pressure (motivation) is fine — you just need to find and straighten the kink (the limiting simplicity factor).
💡 Ask: "What is the one thing making this action hardest for my user right now?" Fix that one thing. Don't add features — remove friction.
Cognitive Heuristics (Brain Shortcuts)

Humans make dozens of snap decisions daily using mental shortcuts — heuristics. Product designers can leverage these to boost perceived motivation or ability without changing the product's actual functionality.

Scarcity Effect

Items perceived as scarce are valued more highly. Amazon's "Only 3 left in stock" nudges purchase decisions even when inventory is abundant.

Framing Effect

Context changes perception. Joshua Bell playing violin in a subway station got ignored; the same music in Carnegie Hall earns $200 tickets. Frame your product in the right context.

Anchoring Effect

The first number seen becomes a reference point. "30% off" anchors on the original price — even if an un-discounted alternative is actually cheaper.

Endowed Progress Effect

People work harder toward a goal they feel they've already started. Car-wash punch cards pre-stamped with 2 free punches had 82% higher completion than blank cards requiring the same 8 purchases.

💡 LinkedIn's profile completion bar is pre-loaded with some progress. You're not starting from zero — you're already partway there. Pure psychology, zero engineering cost.

Stories & Case Studies

Twitter's Homepage Evolution: From Chaos to Clarity

In 2009, Twitter's homepage was a wall of text, links, and explanations — high brain cycles required. User growth stalled. The company iterated relentlessly, asking: what is our users' scarcest resource? By 2012, the homepage had been stripped to two words and two buttons: Sign In / Sign Up. The product description was exactly 140 characters. A single evocative image replaced paragraphs of explanation. Twitter had discovered that no amount of persuasion text converted better than simply making the first action frictionless. The lesson: motivation is what users arrive with. Ability is what you give them.

The Infinite Scroll: Pinterest Removes the Page Break

Before infinite scroll, every website required users to click "next page" and wait for a reload — a tiny but real friction point that interrupted the browsing flow and gave users a natural exit. Pinterest eliminated that pause entirely. As a user nears the bottom, new content loads automatically, and partially visible images at the edge of the screen create just enough curiosity to keep scrolling. The action of consuming content was reduced to a single effortless gesture repeated indefinitely. One small friction removed; engagement multiplied dramatically.

Chapter Takeaways

  • Start with ability, not motivation — reducing friction is faster and cheaper than increasing desire.
  • Map every step from trigger to reward and eliminate any that aren't strictly necessary.
  • Identify which of the 6 simplicity factors is your users' biggest bottleneck right now and fix only that.
  • Use the endowed progress effect: give users a head-start on any goal to increase completion rates.
  • Frame your product in contexts that elevate its perceived value — environment shapes perception as much as quality does.

Apply This Week

▶ Action 1: Walk the full path from your product's trigger to its first reward. Count every tap, click, field, and decision. Then cut at least two steps. If you can't cut them, ask why they exist.

▶ Action 2: Pick one heuristic (scarcity, framing, anchoring, or endowed progress) and design one small experiment to apply it to your onboarding flow this week. Measure whether it changes the first key action rate.
