Chapter 2: Trigger

Concept-Heavy ⏱ ~10 min read

The Big Idea

Every habit begins with a trigger — a cue that initiates behavior. Triggers come in two forms: external (cues in the environment) and internal (cues encoded in memory and emotion). The ultimate goal of any habit-forming product is to move users from needing external triggers to responding to internal ones, so that the product becomes the automatic go-to whenever a specific feeling arises.

Key Concepts

External Triggers

External triggers are sensory cues in the user's environment that tell them what to do next. They carry embedded information — a button, a notification, a link — that points to a single clear action. The best external triggers reduce choice and friction to near zero.

Think of it like: a road sign. It doesn't explain why you should turn — it just tells you to turn now.
In practice: Mint.com's account-alert email reduces the entire page to one action: a large orange "Log In to Mint" button. Every other option is removed. One trigger, one action.
💡 More choices = more hesitation. A trigger that points to one action is more powerful than a trigger pointing to five.
The 4 Types of External Triggers

Not all external triggers are equal. They sit on a spectrum from expensive-but-controllable to free-but-unpredictable:

1. Paid Triggers

Ads, SEM. Effective but costly. Unsustainable as a sole re-engagement strategy.

2. Earned Triggers

PR, viral videos, App Store features. Free but short-lived and unpredictable.

3. Relationship Triggers

Word-of-mouth, referrals, social sharing. Highly effective — can create viral hypergrowth.

4. Owned Triggers

Push notifications, app icons, email lists. User-consented. The engine of repeat engagement until internal triggers form.

💡 Paid and earned get users in the door. Owned triggers keep them coming back. Internal triggers make them stay forever.
Internal Triggers

Internal triggers are emotions — usually negative ones — stored in memory that prompt automatic behavior. When a product becomes tightly associated with relieving a specific feeling (boredom, loneliness, anxiety, FOMO), users reach for it without any external prompt. This is the brass ring of habit design.

Think of it like: scratching an itch. You don't decide to scratch — the discomfort fires the action before conscious thought kicks in.
In practice: Yin opens Instagram every time she fears a moment will be lost forever. She doesn't wait for a notification. The internal trigger — fear of loss — fires automatically.
💡 The most powerful internal triggers are negative emotions: boredom → Twitter, loneliness → Facebook, uncertainty → Google. Build your product as the relief.
The 5 Whys Method (Finding the Real Trigger)

Users can't tell you their internal triggers because they don't consciously know them. The 5 Whys technique — borrowed from Toyota's manufacturing process — drills past declared preferences down to the emotional root cause. Ask "why?" five times about a user behavior. By the fifth answer, you'll have an emotion.

In practice: See the Email example table below — starting from "send messages" and ending at "fear of being out of the loop." That fear is the internal trigger to design for.
💡 People don't use products — they use products to feel something. Keep asking "why" until you hit a feeling.

Stories & Case Studies

Instagram: From Pearl to Habit

Yin, a Stanford student, doesn't think of herself as addicted to Instagram. She just uses it "whenever I see something cool." But what she's describing is a fully-formed internal trigger: the fear that a moment will disappear forever fires before she's consciously aware of it. Her habit started with an external trigger — a friend's recommendation — and was sustained through owned triggers like push notifications and the app icon on her home screen. Over months of repetition, the external scaffolding became unnecessary. The internal trigger — fear of missing out — does all the work now. Instagram didn't just build an app; it colonized an emotion.

Chapter Takeaways

  • Design owned triggers first — notifications, emails, icons — to drive repeat engagement until internal triggers form.
  • Run the 5 Whys on your target user to uncover the emotional root cause behind their behavior.
  • Reduce every external trigger to a single, unmistakable call to action — eliminate competing options.
  • Target negative emotions as internal triggers: they fire more frequently and urgently than positive ones.
  • Write a user narrative: "Every time the user feels [emotion], they [first action]." If you can't complete it, keep researching.

Apply This Week

▶ Action 1: Pick one user persona for your product and run the full 5 Whys on their primary behavior. Write each answer down. The emotion you land on at Why #5 is your internal trigger — design your product to relieve that specific feeling.

▶ Action 2: Audit your current external triggers. For each one, count how many actions the user could take. If it's more than one, simplify. A good trigger leaves exactly one obvious next step.
